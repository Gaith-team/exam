🩺 Hypertension — Definition & BP Classification

📖 Definition ⭐ EXAM

Persistent elevation of systolic or diastolic pressure above 140/90 mmHg, based on two or more measurements.

Called the "Silent Killer" — often symptom-free.

BP = Cardiac Output × Peripheral Resistance

🏷️ Classification of Hypertension ⭐ EXAM

1️⃣ Primary (Essential) HTN

> 90% of all cases — No identifiable cause
Multifactorial, polygenic condition.

Contributing factors:
  • ↑ Sodium-retaining hormones + vasoconstriction
  • Increased sodium intake
  • Central abdominal obesity
  • Diabetes mellitus + excessive alcohol

2️⃣ Secondary HTN

Due to an underlying disease:
  • Congenital narrowing of the aorta
  • Renal: artery stenosis, failure, glomerulonephritis
  • Endocrine: hyperaldosteronism, hypo/hyperthyroidism
  • Neurologic: brain tumor, head injury
  • Medications: cocaine, NSAIDs
  • Pregnancy-induced HTN

3️⃣ Isolated Systolic HTN (ISH) ⭐ EXAM

Systolic ≥ 140 mmHg BUT diastolic < 90 mmHg
Occurs primarily in people > 50 years
Cause: Atherosclerosis → ↓ blood vessel elasticity

4️⃣ Malignant HTN ⭐ EXAM

Emergency condition — diastolic > 120 mmHg
Features: retinal hemorrhage, exudates, rapid vascular deterioration
Without treatment → 90% mortality (renal/CHF)
Causes: Untreated HTN, Eclampsia, toxic substances

  • Heart LV hypertrophy, angina, MI, HF
  • Brain Stroke (CVA) or TIA
  • Kidney Chronic kidney disease
  • Eyes Retinopathy
  • Vessels Peripheral arterial disease

🌡️ Clinical Manifestations ⭐ EXAM

⚠️ Silent Killer — Stages of Symptoms

Early Stage
No symptoms — only elevated BP
As it Advances
Morning occipital headache, fatigue, dizziness, flushing, blurred vision, epistaxis
Advanced (Encephalopathy)
Restlessness, ↓ consciousness, blurred vision, nausea/vomiting, renal insufficiency, proteinuria, hematuria, LV failure, pulmonary edema
⭐ Prolonged uncontrolled HTN outcomes: MI · Heart Failure · Renal Failure · Stroke · Impaired Vision · LV Hypertrophy

💊 Management ⭐ EXAM

🌿 Non-Pharmacologic (DASH) ⭐ EXAM

Goal BP: ≤140/90 (≤130/80 for DM/CKD)
  • Weight reduction
  • Restrict alcohol and sodium
  • Regular exercise + relaxation/meditation
  • DASH diet — ↑ fruits, vegetables, low-fat dairy

💊 First-Line Drug Classes ⭐ EXAM

1. Diuretics 2. Beta-Blockers
Diuretics:
• Thiazide (Diuril) — inexpensive, oral, mild SE
  ⚠️ CI: Gout, renal impairment, hyponatremia
  SE: Dry mouth, weakness, tachycardia, postural hypotension
  🔑 Monitor: electrolytes, weight, VS; K-rich foods

• Loop Diuretic (Lasix) — rapid action, used when thiazide fails
  SE: Volume depletion, electrolyte loss

Beta-Blockers (Propranolol):
  ⚠️ CI: Asthma, CHF, diabetes, HR <60 bpm, heart block
  SE: Depression, insomnia, lightheadedness
  🔑 Check heart rate before giving!

🩺 Nursing Process — Hypertension ⭐ EXAM

I. Assessment

  • BP at frequent intervals — know baseline
  • Signs of target organ damage (angina, SOB, speech/vision changes, headache, nosebleed, nocturia)
  • Apical & peripheral pulse rate, rhythm, character
  • Personal/social/financial impact of HTN

II. Nursing Diagnoses

  • Deficient knowledge r/t treatment regimen & disease control
  • Noncompliance with therapeutic regimen r/t side effects

III. Goals

  • Understanding disease & treatment
  • Participation in self-care program

IV. Nursing Interventions ⭐ EXAM

1. Education
Control (not cure) HTN. Dietitian consult. Restrict sodium & fat, ↑ fruits/veg. Takes 2–3 months for taste buds to adapt to ↓ salt.
2. Self-Care Teaching
Home BP monitoring. Written info on drug side effects. No sudden stop of meds (rebound HTN!). Adequate medication supply.
3. Adherence
Small changes each visit. Support groups for weight, smoking, stress. Exercise regimen. Family support. Follow-up visits.

❤️‍🩹 Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD)

📖 Definitions ⭐ EXAM

Rheumatic Fever (RF): Autoimmune inflammatory reaction to Streptococcus pyogenes (Group A beta-hemolytic Strep) throat/skin infection.
Affects: Heart (carditis), large joints (arthritis), brain (chorea)

Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD): Structural/functional changes in heart due to valve damage + muscle inflammation from one or more RF episodes. Life-threatening.

⚠️ Risk Factors for RHD

  • Environmental: Overcrowding, poor sanitation, poor access to healthcare/antibiotics
  • Bacteria: Certain strep strains more likely to cause RF, especially repeated untreated infections
  • Genetics: Genes that increase susceptibility to RF

🏥 Diagnostic Criteria — Modified Jones Criteria ⭐ EXAM

Diagnosis = 2 Major OR 1 Major + 2 Minor OR 3 Minor criteria
MAJOR Criteria:
  • Carditis (clinical or subclinical)
  • Polyarthritis
  • Chorea — jerky uncontrollable movements (hands, feet, face)
  • Subcutaneous nodules — small, painless bumps
  • Erythema marginatum — flat/raised painless rash with ragged edge
MINOR Criteria:
  • Fever ≥ 38.5°C (≥38°C in high-risk)
  • Polyarthralgia — painful joints (knees, ankles, elbows, wrists)
  • Prolonged PR interval on ECG
  • ↑ ESR ≥60 mm/h and/or CRP ≥3.0 mg/dL

🔬 Diagnosis Tests ⭐ EXAM

  • Throat culture/blood test — check for strep
  • Echocardiogram — most useful — valve damage, regurgitation, fluid, enlargement
  • ECG — arrhythmias, heart muscle damage
  • Chest X-ray — cardiac enlargement
  • Cardiac MRI — detailed valve/muscle images
  • Blood tests — infection + inflammation

💊 Treatment ⭐ EXAM

  • Antibiotics (Penicillin) — treat strep & prevent RF
  • Anti-inflammatory drugs (aspirin, steroids, NSAIDs)
  • Anticoagulants — reduce stroke/clot risk (mitral stenosis, AFib)
  • Daily/monthly antibiotic prophylaxis
  • Surgery (severe cases): valvuloplasty or valve replacement
Best treatment = PREVENT rheumatic fever early!

⚠️ Complications of RHD ⭐ EXAM

Valve Stenosis
Valve flaps thick/stiff → ↓ blood flow
Regurgitation
Valve flaps don't close → backward blood flow
Muscle Damage
Swelling weakens heart muscle → ↓ pumping
Heart Failure
Chronic damage → HF later in life
Atrial Fibrillation
Irregular, fast heartbeat
Pulmonary HTN
↑ stroke/clot risk

🛡️ Prevention Levels ⭐ EXAM

Primordial
Address poverty, improve sanitation, reduce crowding, ↑ healthcare access
Primary
Diagnose & treat strep pharyngitis/skin infections promptly with antibiotics
Secondary
Continuous antibiotic prophylaxis for RF/RHD patients to prevent recurrence
Antibiotic prophylaxis: Amoxicillin 2g orally 1 hour before dental/respiratory/urinary procedures. Allergy: use clindamycin or cephalexin.
🩺 Nursing Management — RHD

II. Nursing Diagnoses

  • Impaired cardiac function r/t valvular damage
  • Risk for infection r/t recurrent strep
  • Noncompliance r/t knowledge deficit
  • Activity intolerance r/t ↓ cardiac output
  • Risk for thromboembolism r/t valvular damage
  • Knowledge deficit r/t RHD self-care

IV. Nursing Interventions ⭐ EXAM

  • Assessment: heart murmurs, VS, HF signs
  • Medications: antibiotics, anti-inflammatories, diuretics
  • Pain management
  • Education: complete antibiotic course, healthy lifestyle
  • Activity/Rest balance
  • Nutrition: ↓ sodium, heart-healthy diet
  • Fluid management: diuretics, I&O monitoring
  • Emotional support
  • Infection control: hand hygiene
  • Surgical consultation if severe valve damage
